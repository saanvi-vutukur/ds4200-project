Enterprise Adoption of AI Tools

The rapid adoption of generative AI tools across industries has raised pressing questions about the uniformity of their impact. Although tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are broadly accessible, the extent to which firms integrate these technologies — and the outcomes they experience — varies significantly by sector. Industries such as Finance and Technology may invest in different tools, commit varying levels of training, and see distinct workforce outcomes compared to sectors like Healthcare. Understanding these differences is essential for business leaders and policymakers seeking to maximize the benefits of GenAI while addressing disparities in productivity and workforce impact. This analysis draws on a filtered subset of over 7,000 US-based enterprise observations spanning 14 industries. Through five visualizations, we examine productivity differences across sectors, workforce outcome distributions, and how company size shapes the return on AI training investment.

Saanvi Vutukur, Maya Korintholil, Aashka Dave

The data

Data description

Dataset: Enterprise GenAI Adoption and Workforce Impact (Kaggle). Each row represents a single enterprise's self-reported survey response. The original dataset contains 100,000 observations across 10 columns; we filtered to US-based enterprises only, yielding approximately 7,000 rows spanning 14 industries.

Key Columns: Industry, Company Size, GenAI Tool used, Adoption Year, Number of Employees Impacted, New Roles Created, Training Hours Provided, Productivity Change (%), and Employee Sentiment. We engineered three additional features: Years Since Adoption, Training Hours per Employee, and Training ROI. This brings the total to 13 columns with a strong mix of categorical and continuous variables.

Limitations: The dataset is survey-based, so productivity change figures are self-reported and may reflect perceived rather than objectively measured outcomes. The dataset does not distinguish between tool usage intensity (occasional vs. deep integration), and the US-only filter, while intentional, limits generalizability. Some industries have slightly unequal sample sizes, which may affect cross-sector comparisons.

Visualizations

Figure 1 · Average Productivity Impact by Industry
This dot plot shows the average self-reported productivity change (%) for each of the 14 industries in our dataset, with a dashed vertical line marking the overall mean of approximately 18.6%. Technology leads all sectors with an average productivity gain near 19.3%, followed by Retail and Telecom. Entertainment, Healthcare, and Finance cluster at the lower end, notably below the mean. The spread across industries is relatively narrow (roughly 1 percentage point separates the lowest from the highest), suggesting that GenAI provides productivity benefits broadly, but the degree of benefit is meaningfully differentiated by sector.

Figure 2 · Productivity Impact with Standard Error
Adding standard error bars reveals important nuance beyond the point estimates in Figure 1. Industries like Technology and Retail not only have higher average productivity gains, but their confidence intervals do not overlap with those of lower-performing sectors like Entertainment and Healthcare. This suggests that these differences are statistically meaningful. Conversely, industries such as Hospitality, Education, and Legal Services show wide error bars, indicating high variability in enterprise experiences within those sectors. This suggests that for some industries, the benefit of GenAI adoption depends heavily on firm-specific factors such as implementation quality or workforce readiness.

Figure 3 · Workforce Impact Distribution by Industry
This stacked bar chart shows what proportion of companies in each industry experienced each of four workforce outcomes: “Reskilling & Growth”, “Role Change”, “No Major Impact”, and “Job Loss Risk”. Across all industries, "No Major Impact" is the dominant outcome, accounting for roughly 55–60% of enterprises. “Reskilling & Growth” is the second most common outcome (around 38–43%), while “Job Loss Risk” remains the smallest category across all sectors. Healthcare and Transportation show slightly elevated “Reskilling & Growth” proportions, while Finance and Hospitality show slightly higher “Job Loss Risk” compared to peers. Overall, the chart suggests that GenAI adoption is more often a catalyst for workforce evolution than displacement. n

Figure 4 · Training ROI by Company Size
This bar chart demonstrates that the return on GenAI training investment scales dramatically with company size. Small companies report an average Training ROI near 50, while medium companies reach approximately 185, large companies approximately 460, and enterprise-scale organizations approach 740. This likely reflects economies of scale in AI deployment, where larger firms can spread training costs across more employees, benefit from deeper infrastructure integration, and absorb the upfront investment more efficiently. The interactive industry filter allows users to verify that this pattern holds broadly across sectors, with some industries (like Transportation and Utilities) showing even steeper enterprise-size premiums.

Summary & findings

Unequal Productivity Gains Across Sectors

Productivity gains from GenAI are real but unequal. Technology, Retail, and Telecom see the strongest average improvements, while Entertainment, Healthcare, and Finance lag behind. This suggests that sector-specific barriers (regulatory constraints, workflow complexity, or data sensitivity) moderate how much productivity value enterprises extract from these tools.

Reskilling Dominates Over Job Loss

The dominant workforce outcome of GenAI adoption is not displacement but evolution. "No Major Impact" and "Reskilling & Growth" together account for the vast majority of outcomes in every industry. Job Loss Risk is present but consistently the smallest category, countering the most alarming narratives around AI and employment.

Size Drives Training ROI

Company size is the single strongest predictor of Training ROI. Enterprise-scale organizations extract nearly 15 times the training return of small companies. This gap suggests that policymakers and smaller firms should focus on scaling training programs and sharing best practices to close the size-based advantage gap.

Future work

Future work could examine how these patterns shift as adoption matures beyond 2024, explore differences between specific GenAI tools (e.g., ChatGPT vs. Copilot), or incorporate objective productivity measures rather than self-reported estimates.
